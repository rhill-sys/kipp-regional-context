KIPP Regional Performance in Context

A normalized look at KIPP TEAM & Family (Newark · Camden · Miami), KIPP New Orleans, KIPP Public Schools Northern California, and KIPP NYC — using the measures that are designed to travel across state lines (CSGF Similar Students Measure and student growth percentiles), plus test-difficulty, funding, and market-structure context that raw proficiency comparisons leave out.

The headline: on the fairest common yardstick, these regions are near the top of the network

KIPP TEAM & Family's students out-grew KIPP NYC's students in both of the last two years (SGP 74.9 vs 48.7 in 2023-24; 69.3 vs 58.8 in 2024-25). New York's moving yardstick

Between 2013 and 2025, New York State's reported 3-8 proficiency rose by ~22 points in ELA and ~24 points in math. Over the same period, the state's own raw-score bar for "proficient" fell (from 68% to 52% of available points in math; 70% to 59% in ELA), NYSED declared results non-comparable across at least four breaks — and New York's NAEP scores declined. Any KIPP NYC trend line drawn across this period inherits that drift.

KIPP NYC's headline claim for 2015-2023 — math proficiency 55%→74% — spans three of the four breaks above, during which statewide math went from 38% to 50%+ on progressively easier scoring. Not all "proficient" is created equal

NCES's mapping study places every state's proficiency cut on the common NAEP scale. In the most recent mapping (2022 — the last before NY re-anchored its baseline to post-pandemic performance), New Jersey's grade-4 math bar sat at the NAEP Proficient line itself, 13 points above New York's. A Newark student and a Bronx student called "proficient" are not clearing the same bar.

This context reframes the regional comparisons. KIPP New Jersey posts SSM 83.8 against the hardest proficiency bar of these five states. KIPP New Orleans operates in the state producing the nation's best-documented post-COVID recovery — a rising bar, not a falling one. New York, meanwhile, re-set its baseline to 2022's depressed performance and has posted state-test gains while its NAEP scores fell. A network formula that projects "+12 points a year" off NY-style score movement is projecting, in part, the behavior of New York's scale — not a transferable instructional effect.

The resource and market context NYC comparisons omit

Even a perfectly normalized test comparison would still ignore what it costs to produce a point of achievement in each city. The differences are not marginal — they are structural.

Structural conditions, side by side

Why per-proficiency-point comparisons across these regions are not apples to apples.

New Orleans, Newark, and Camden operate under universal enrollment (OneApp/NCAP, Newark Enrolls, Camden's unified system): centralized lotteries, mandatory backfill, mid-year intake of highly mobile students. NYC charters run their own lotteries and are not required to backfill upper grades. Research consistently shows backfill and mid-year mobility depress measured proficiency. This is a design difference, not an excuse — the regions carrying it should not be scored as if it didn't exist.

Within their own cities, against their own peers

The comparison the network narrative skips: how each region stacks up in its own market, most recent public year.

One more inconvenient symmetry: in its own market, KIPP NYC's 2024 ELA (55.2%) ran below the NYC charter-sector average (58.2%), and Success Academy — operating under the identical NY test, funding, and labor conditions — ranked #1 in math among 700+ NY districts and networks in 2025. If within-market rank is the standard the Foundation wants to apply to New Orleans and Newark, it is not a standard that flatters New York either.

The base-rate test: what the goals actually asked for

A regional academic-office analysis of Louisiana's public LEAP data (grades 3-8; internal computation, July 2026, checkable against LDOE files) tested the Foundation's growth goals — +13.5 points in ELA and +12.5 in math — against every district and school in the state. The goals were derived from growth rates that individual schools somewhere in the KIPP network hit the prior year, then applied to regions as averages.

The aggregation fallacy: extreme single-school growth is largely variance — small cohorts, one grade configuration, sometimes composition change — and variance shrinks mechanically as portfolios aggregate, so the 90th percentile of school growth always sits far above the 90th percentile of district growth. Expecting a region to average its best member's outlier year is statistically incoherent — and the Foundation's updated methodology carries the same flaw, knowingly unaddressed. The concrete cost: a leader running the #1 school in New Orleans among schools serving >80% economically disadvantaged students would be classified a low performer under these goals. Orleans is the only Louisiana district on the state's most-improved list four consecutive years — up 19 ranking spots, outperforming peer large districts for the second time since the 1980s — and no CMO in the city has grown at the goal rate in any of those four years. A region can triple the state's growth rate, lead its city among high-poverty schools, and still be scored a failure. That is a property of the yardstick, not of the schools.

What this analysis does not claim

Credibility requires conceding what the data actually shows.

  • KIPP NYC is a strong system. Top-3 SSM, +14 to +19 points over its district in math, network-leading APR. The claim here is narrower: its edge over KTAF/NorCal is modest once state context is held constant, its growth is mid-pack, and its trend line is inflated by scale drift no school caused.
  • KIPP New Orleans's absolute levels are genuinely low (SSM 49.0 in 24-25, down from 61.1) and below the Orleans Parish average. The defensible story is trajectory — the D→C→A letter-grade recovery, math roughly doubling from the 2021-22 trough at most schools, in the highest-poverty student body in the network (94.7% SED) — not the level.
  • These are 21-22 CMOs, two years of SSM data. Descriptive, not causal. Weighted-average rollups of school percentiles slightly understate the pooled CMO figures. YoY moves at small CMOs are noisy.
  • Growth comparisons cut both ways: much of the rest-of-network's 24-25 SSM gain came from low-baseline regions bouncing (Columbus 16→80, OKC, NC, Capital Region +14-20). Symmetrically, KTAF's "slower growth" reflects being near the ceiling — there is limited headroom above the 82nd percentile.
